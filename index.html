ADHD Collateral Informant Questionnaire

Structured collateral information in support of a DIVA-5 informed diagnostic interview for Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

This questionnaire has been provided to you by Soumya Psychology as part of a comprehensive neurodevelopmental assessment (DIVA-5, Diagnostic Interview for ADHD in Adults, version 5). Your responses form part of the clinical collateral information used, alongside direct interview and standardised measures, to inform diagnostic formulation. Please answer as accurately and specifically as possible, drawing on your direct observation of the client. If you have questions about how this information will be used, please contact the practice before returning this form.

1. Client & informant details

2. Purpose of this form

The following items reflect DSM-5-TR diagnostic criteria for ADHD, structured to align with the DIVA-5 interview framework. For each behaviour, tick whether you currently observe this (or have observed it in the past six months), and whether it was also present in childhood (before age 12). Please add a brief concrete example where possible; specific examples are more useful diagnostically than general impressions.

Not every informant will have known the client during childhood, for example a spouse or partner. If this applies to you, please mark “Unsure” (or leave blank) for the Childhood column and focus on the Current column and your own direct observations. Your input is still valuable even without childhood knowledge.

Autism Collateral Informant Questionnaire

Structured collateral information in support of a MIGDAS-2 informed assessment for Autism Spectrum presentations

This questionnaire has been provided to you by Soumya Psychology as part of a comprehensive neurodevelopmental assessment (MIGDAS-2, Monteiro Interview Guidelines for Diagnosing the Autism Spectrum, 2nd edition). Your responses form part of the clinical collateral information used, alongside direct interview and observation of the client, to inform diagnostic formulation. Please respond with specific, concrete examples rather than general impressions where possible.

1. Client & informant details

2. Purpose of this form

The MIGDAS-2 approach gathers information across communication, social relating, sensory experience, emotional regulation, and interests/repetitive behaviours. Your perspective as someone who knows the client well is a valuable source of information across these same domains.

3. Communication & conversation style

AreaObservations / examples
Preferred topics of conversation: what does the client most enjoy talking about, and how much do they steer conversation toward these topics?
Reciprocity: does conversation flow back and forth, or tend to be one-sided / monologue-like?
Use of language: tone, rhythm, formality, scripting or repeated phrases, literal interpretation
Non-verbal communication: eye contact, facial expression, gesture use, body language
Understanding of unwritten social rules (e.g. turn-taking, personal space, sarcasm/humour)

4. Social relationships & peer interaction

AreaObservations / examples
Friendships: number, depth, stability over time, how they are formed and maintained
Preferred social style: one-to-one vs group, parallel play/activity vs interactive play
Understanding of others’ perspectives, intentions, or unstated feelings
Response to social overtures from peers (approach, avoidance, misreading intent)
Behaviour in unstructured social situations (e.g. recess, social events, break times)

5. Sensory experiences

Please note any sensory sensitivities (aversive reactions) or sensory seeking behaviours across the following domains.

Sensory domainObservations / examples
Auditory: reactions to loud/unexpected sounds, background noise, specific sounds sought or avoided
Visual: sensitivity to light, visual patterns/fascinations, avoidance of visual clutter
Tactile: reactions to touch, clothing textures/tags, food textures, preference for/aversion to certain textures
Taste & smell: restricted diet, strong reactions to smells or tastes
Vestibular / proprioceptive: movement seeking (spinning, rocking, jumping) or avoidance; body awareness in space
Pain / temperature: unusual responses to pain, heat, or cold

6. Emotional regulation & flexibility

AreaObservations / examples
Emotional regulation: how the client manages frustration, anxiety, or overwhelm
Meltdowns / shutdowns: frequency, typical triggers, presentation, recovery time
Response to change: transitions between activities, changes to routine or plans
Flexibility of thinking: ability to consider alternatives, tolerate uncertainty, compromise

7. Interests, routines & repetitive behaviours

AreaObservations / examples
Special interests: nature, intensity, and how much time/focus is devoted to them
Repetitive movements or mannerisms (e.g. hand-flapping, rocking, pacing): context and frequency
Insistence on sameness: rigid routines, rituals, need for order or predictability
Play style in earlier childhood: imaginative/symbolic play vs repetitive or object-focused play
